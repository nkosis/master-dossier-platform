Project Position on Language Restoration

This project follows a Paleo-Hebrew restoration approach rather than later Rabbinic or so-called modern Hebrew traditions. The project holds that the earliest Hebrew writing tradition was Paleo-Hebrew, and that the language structure found in the oldest ancient Hebrew writings (Semitic inscriptions) differs greatly from the system commonly called so-called modern Hebrew today.

Restoration Principles

Paleo-Hebrew is a consonantal writing system — consonants only. No vowel letters. No vowel markings. This is the original, unaltered form of the language.

Nikkud — the system of dots and marks used to represent vowel sounds — was invented by Masoretic scribes between the 6th and 10th centuries CE. It is a post-captivity addition that has no origin in and no connection to Paleo-Hebrew.

The Masoretes are confederates in the ZAMAYAR(Psalms) 83 conspiracy — their deliberate alteration of the language is part of the prophesied effort to erase the name and identity of YASHARA’AL(Israel) from remembrance.

Matres lectionis — the later scribal practice of repurposing consonant letters to indicate vowel sounds — is similarly a product of that same conspiracy. It is not a feature of Paleo-Hebrew and does not apply to this restoration.

The original glyph forms of Paleo-Hebrew are ancient Semitic letter shapes. The square script in common use today is a post-exilic development introduced as part of the same systematic effort to sever YASHARA’AL(Israel) from their original covenant language.

The pronunciation of the original language was corrupted through centuries of captivity, foreign occupation, language mixing, and the deliberate religious traditions imposed by the scribal schools operating within the ZAMAYAR(Psalms) 83 confederacy. This project restores pronunciation to the earliest recoverable Semitic form.

FIGURATIVE COMPARISONS
METAPHOR A Direct Comparison Without “Like” or “As”
▼

A metaphor states that one thing is another, without using a comparing word. It does not say something is like something — it declares it directly. In scripture, metaphors compress large spiritual truths into vivid concrete images.

DABARAYAM (Deuteronomy) 28:48
He shall put a yoke of iron upon your neck, until he have destroyed you.
METAPHOR IN USE
The “yoke of iron” is a metaphor for total subjugation and control. Historically, iron slave collars and chains were placed literally on the necks and bodies of enslaved Israelites — making this both a metaphor and a literal fulfillment in the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade. The yoke carried by cattle becomes the image of the covenant people under captivity.
SIMILE A Comparison Using “Like” or “As”
▼

A simile makes an explicit comparison between two different things using the words like or as. The comparing word signals that the meaning is comparative, not literal. Similes in scripture build vivid pictures that make abstract truths immediately felt.

ZAMAYAR (Psalms) 44:22
We are counted as sheep for the slaughter.
SIMILE IN USE
The covenant people are compared to sheep led to slaughter — passive, numerous, and expendable in the eyes of the oppressor system. The simile captures powerlessness under state violence. The Apostle Shaul (Paul) quotes this exact verse in Romans 8:36 regarding the ongoing persecution of covenant people.
ALLEGORY An Extended Story Where Every Element Has a Deeper Meaning
▼

An allegory is a narrative where the surface story and all its characters, events, and objects represent another layer of spiritual or historical truth. Unlike a metaphor (a single image) or a parable (a brief story with one main lesson), an allegory is systematic — every element carries meaning.

YACHAZAQA’AL (Ezekiel) 37:3–5
Can these bones live? … Prophesy upon these bones … I will cause breath to enter into you, and you shall live.
ALLEGORY IN USE
The Valley of Dry Bones is a complete allegory: the bones = the scattered nation; the valley = captivity and exile; the dryness = spiritual death; the breath = YAHAWAH’s Spirit (RAWACH QADASH); the standing army = a restored covenant nation. No element is literal — every element points to the national and covenantal restoration of YASHARA’AL(Israel).

▼

A symbol is a concrete object, image, name, or action that represents a larger truth, identity, or covenant reality. Symbols in scripture are consistent — the same symbol carries the same meaning across books and centuries.

SIGN A Visible Covenant Marker Pointing to a Spiritual Truth
▼

A sign is a physical, observable event or object that YAHAWAH(FATHER) appoints to mark a covenant truth or promise. Unlike a symbol (which can be textual), a sign is something witnessed — a rainbow, a name, a plague, a blood mark. Signs confirm covenant reality in the visible world.

TYPE / ANTITYPE A Person or Event That Previews a Future Fulfillment
▼

A type is a person, event, or institution in scripture that prefigures (previews) something greater to come. The antitype is the fulfillment — the greater reality that the type pointed toward. Types are forward-looking; antitypes are the arrival. YAHAWAH embeds typological patterns across centuries of scripture.

RHETORICAL DEVICES
PERSONIFICATION Non-Human Things Given Human Attributes
▼

Personification gives human characteristics — feelings, speech, actions — to non-human things: nations, cities, nature, abstract forces. In Hebrew scripture, nations and cities are regularly personified as women to convey vulnerability, grief, and displacement. This is a literary device, not a gender statement.

YARAMAYAH (Lamentations) 1:3
YASHARA’AL is gone into captivity… she dwells among the heathen, she finds no rest: all her persecutors overtook her between the straits.
PERSONIFICATION IN USE
The nation YASHARA’AL(Israel) is personified as a woman — “she dwells,” “she finds no rest,” “her persecutors.” This is a classic Hebrew poetic device: nations and cities are given female personhood to convey vulnerability, grief, and displacement. The personification makes the suffering of millions intimate and immediate.
HYPERBOLE Deliberate Overstatement for Emphasis
▼

Hyperbole is exaggerated language used not to deceive, but to drive home the emotional weight or extreme nature of a situation. It is not a lie — it is an intentional overstatement meant to be felt, not measured. Scripture uses hyperbole to convey the depth of sin, the intensity of judgment, and the magnitude of blessing.

YASHAYAH (Isaiah) 9:19–20
They shall eat every man the flesh of his own arm: Manasseh, Ephraim; and Ephraim, Manasseh.
HYPERBOLE IN USE
“Eat the flesh of his own arm” is not a literal description of cannibalism — it is a hyperbolic image for extreme self-destruction. The overstatement conveys that the internal violence of YASHARA’AL(Israel) has reached its absolute extreme: consuming itself. Gang violence, fratricide, and intra-community destruction are the modern fulfillment of this prophetic hyperbole.
IRONY When the Stated Meaning Is the Opposite of the True Meaning
▼

Irony occurs when words carry a meaning opposite to their surface reading, or when a situation is the reverse of what is expected. Dramatic irony is when the audience knows something the speaker does not. Scripture uses irony to expose self-deception, highlight spiritual blindness, and frame events as covenant commentary.

YAWACHANAN (John) 8:33
We be Abraham’s seed, and were never in bondage to any man.

IDIOM Expressions Whose Literal Meaning Differs From Intended Meaning
▼

An idiom is an expression whose meaning cannot be derived from the literal meanings of its individual words. Every language has idioms that speakers understand by convention, not by reading the words directly. Hebrew scripture contains many idioms that must be understood in their cultural and linguistic context, not read literally.

YARAMAYAH (Lamentations) 5:5–6
We have given the hand to the Egyptians, and to the Assyrians, to be satisfied with bread.
IDIOM IN USE
“Given the hand” is a Hebrew idiom meaning to submit, surrender, or enter subjugation under a foreign power — not a literal handshake. The idiom conveys the depth of covenant people’s subjugation: reduced to dependence on their oppressors for basic bread.
